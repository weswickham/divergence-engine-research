## 5. Open Reflection

>Now that I understand how the settings will influence the outputs, I am better able to use the tool itself and achieve results that are starting to become more actionable ideation sparks. For instance the reference depth - when set to 'concrete' will yield sparks that are more likely to be directly intrepretable as visual sparks (for example, describing concrete 'things') whereas setting that to 'abstract' can yield interesting results that are more conceptual in nature. The register, or tone of voice is also critical. When set to 'common' or 'everyday' the results are likley to yield low lexical vocabulary. The similar finding from previous tests is still critically relevant - which is the 'novelty' setting - remembering that rare/novel can push the model to odd, strange and only tangentially related outputs. 

>It is important to remember the purpose of the DE and why it is being used. It can produce words, phrases that solve immediate problems (create a name for this thing) but it has been developed to explore divergent thinking and early stage linguistic ideation by creating fragements and sparks on given themes. It is easy to fall into the trap of considering the outputs as 'solutions' to the greater communication problem. 

>It is equally important to frame up the brief as explicitly as possible.

>Quantity does not equate to more quality. 20 fragment counts is a manageable quantity for me to quickly review for strong sparks. Increasing the quantity (eg. to 50) will almost certainly trigger the velvet problem without considerable steering effort.
